Glassware

Pilsner Glass

The pilsner glass is descended from the old beer flute, as used in the 1600s and 1700s. Tall and narrow, it displays the beer’s carbonation to excellent effect and allows for a good head.

Beer Mug

The beer mug is the modern descendent of the beer tankard dating back to the Middle Ages. Originally, tankards were made from clay, pewter, silver, wood or leather, and tended to be very large.

Brandy Snifter

In mid-19th century American slang, a “snifter” was a slug of straight spirits. The glass is built wide, squat and short-stemmed, so that it will force the spirit to release the most aroma.

Champagne Coupe

The champagne coupe is older than the flute and dates back to the 1600s. Its wide, shallow bowl is excellent at keeping liquid from sloshing out.

Cocktail Glass

This iconic glass with its V-shape and long stem didn’t begin its career as a Cocktail glass at all, but rather for wine, punches, and later champagne.

Collins Glass

Tall, narrow and straight-sided, the Collins glass is nothing more than a large Fizz or Highball glass- usually holds 14 to 16 ounces.

Champagne Flute

The champagne flute began in Venice, at the end of the 1500s. A tall, slender, stemmed 6- to 8-ounce glass with gently incurving sides the champagne flute is excellent at preserving and displaying the wine’s bubbles.

Highball

Americans started drinking Scotch whisky with soda and ice in the early 1890s, they didn’t have a glass for it. Bartenders reached for a soda glass. Tall and narrow, it was perfect for a jigger of booze, ice, and a squirt of soda.

Hurricane Glass

The Hurricane glass derives its distinctive shape from the hurricane lantern, known to campers everywhere. Take the glass chimney from the lantern, put a round bottom and a stem on it and flare out the rim a little and fill it.

Irish Coffee Glass

Irish Coffee was introduced in the 1950s. It has its own dedicated glass- a version of the Sour glass with a little extra flare at the rim and a ring-handle molded on the side so you can hold it without burning your fingers.

Old Fashioned

The classic Old-Fashioned glass is the modern descendant of the “small bar glass.” Heavy-bottomed and slant-sided, it held 5 to 6 ounces and was used both for mixing and for serving cocktails and other short drinks.

Cordial or Pony glass

The cordial glass dates back to the very beginning of the 19th century when it became customary in gentle company to follow a meal with a few sips of curaçao, maraschino, crème de noyeau or some other rich, sweet liqueur..

Wine Glass

In the late 19th century, the French began migrating to “balloon” or “tulip” glasses— broad in the belly and narrow in the neck. This freed the aromas of the wine and concentrated them so that they could be appreciated. .

Sherry Glass

A sherry glass is a type of drinkware generally used for serving aromatic alcoholic beverages, such as sherry, port, aperitifs, and liqueurs, and layered shooters. An ISO-standard sized sherry glass is 120 millilitres.

Shot glass

A small glass designed to hold or measure alcoholic spirits or liquor, which is either drunk straight from the glass ("a shot") or poured into a cocktail. Alcohol served in a shot glass, typically consumed quickly, is also known as a "shooter."

Sour Glass

Sours begin with a jigger of booze and added citrus juice and a sweetener, and often a red wine float - and it doesn't fit into the standard Cocktail glass. Enter the Sour glass, a narrowish, 5- or 6-ounce glass with slanted sides.

Stein

A large mug used traditionally for beer. Steins may be made out of stoneware, pewter, porcelain, or even silver, wood or crystal glass; they may have open tops or hinged pewter lids with a thumb-lever.

Stem Glass

Stands on stems above a base. It is usually made from glass, but may be made from ceramics or metals. The stem allows the drinker to hold the glass without affecting the temperature of the drink. Usually used for wines.
